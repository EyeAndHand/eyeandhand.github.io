# Champions Brawl
![Image](/assets/images/rpg-00.png)
## Details
**Project Status:** Delisted, as of January 2023

**Project Type:** Indie

**Engine:** Unity

**Primary Role:** Technical Game Designer

**Team Size:** 15

---
Champions Brawl was a mobile auto-chess style title that I had worked on at a mid-sized independent studio. Working with a small team naturally lends itself to wearing many hats, and I had to flex into several positions as team sizes fluxuated throughout development and post-release. Unfortunately, the game was shutdown due to the economic strain on the studio during the COVID-19 quarantines.

The game was built around a core of tactical boardbuilding, with players taking turns placing down their game pieces (during PvE and live PvP) and having thier units automatically fight eachother. We had designed this around the wave of popular autochess titles that had been cropping up around that time, and felt that building on the tactical side of placing and choosing units would allow for gameplay that could continue with minimal need to talk to the server or wait through matchmaking queues. While these design considerations were initially for financial and scope reasons, it created an interesting twist on the genre that players found engaging.
## Combat Design
![Image](/assets/images/rpg-02.png)
Built on the foundations of autochess, it was decided early to remove "alliance" type bonuses to help encourage more creative unit compositions. We also added a "star limit" system, where the player could only have a certain number of "stars" on the board, meaning a player could either build a packed board of weaker units or place a few units that were very powerful. These two decisions necessitated creating units that were more built around actings as tools than as simple attacking units, giving characters specific abilities with predictable effects that allowed players to deeply strategize, but keeping many of the common unit effects simple and readable so as to not alienate more casual players.
## AI Design
![Image](/assets/images/rpg-01.png)
In the early stages of designing the enemy AI it was decided to make it purely deterministic, both to ensure that players would have to meaningfully interact with what the AI was doing and that the outcomes of battles would be entirely deterministic based on the player's input. This had the added effect of dedicated players creating thriving discouse over beating more difficult PvE segments, such as during limited events, by collaborating and strategizing on unit composition and board placement.

Most difficult was getting the AI to both be able to meaningfully react to the player's actions during the placement phase, but also not completely disrupt their own board placement in an attempt to counter the player. This led to our "heat" system, where we calculated "hotzones" on the board where a player's current unit composition would be most dangerous and made the AI choose positions outside of those zones when placing units that would be considered "vulnerable". This also allowed the AI to react to players leaving powerful but vulnerable units exposed, by applying a negative "heat" value in the areas near them.
